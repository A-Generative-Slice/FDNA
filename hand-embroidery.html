More About Hand Embroidery

One of the oldest forms of textile art, used for centuries to personalize clothing and signify status. It allows for greater texture, varying thread thicknesses, and is a slow, meditative process requiring precision.

Common Hand Embroidery Stitches

Basic Line Stitches: Running Stitch, Backstitch, Stem Stitch.
Filling Stitches: Satin Stitch, Long and Short Stitch.
Decorative: French Knots, Lazy Daisy, Chain Stitch, Fishbone, Bullion Knot.

Phulkari of Punjab

"Phulkari means flower work. The design includes not only flowers but also cover motifs and geometrical shapes. The main characteristics are the use of darn stitch on the wrong side of coarse cotton cloth with coloured silken thread."

Kasuti of Karnataka

"Kasuti work is very intricate, sometimes involves putting up to 5,000 stitches by hand and is traditionally made on Ilkal sarees."

Kantha of Bengal

"It is an ancient craft found in West Bengal. The traditional folk art was popular in Bengal. The word itself means 'Patched Cloth'."

Embroidery of Rajasthan

"Rajasthan hand embroidery is a traditional art form that uses fine stitch and motifs to decorate clothing and other items."

Embroidery of Kashmir (Kashida)

"Kashida embroidery which came with Persian, just as the motifs and style suggest much of Persian influence."

Kutch Kathiawar & Sindh Embroidery

"It is characterized with embroidery in bright colours, mirrors, beads and intricate and extensive embroidery that embellishes the entire fabric."

Chicken work [Chikankari] of Lucknow

"Chikankari involves stitching intricate patterns onto light weight fabric like cotton, chiffon, silk and muslin."

Machine Embroidery: Outline

"Machine embroidery is an embroidery process whereby a sewing machine or embroidery machine is used to create patterns on textiles."

Machine Embroidery: Zig Zag Stitch

The Zig Zag stitch is a back-and-forth stitch used primarily for finishing raw edges or creating decorative borders. It is often used for appliqué work or as a faster alternative to hand-worked satin stitches.

Machine Embroidery: Filling

Machine filling involves using dense, closely spaced stitches to cover an entire area of a design with color and texture, creating varied surface effects from smooth finishes to raised patterns.

Machine Embroidery: Couching

Couching is a technique where a decorative cord, yarn, or heavy thread is laid across the fabric surface and fastened down with small, inconspicuous machine stitches. Ideal for bold, 3D outlines.

Styles of Printing

Resist Printing: A paste prevents dye from penetrating specific areas.

Stencil Printing: Applying dye through a cut-out design onto the fabric, allowing for high precision and repetition of complex motifs.

Tie and Dye Printing (Knotting)

"Tie and dye painting is one of the oldest known methods of hand dyeing. Knotting involves physically tying parts of the fabric into knots before immersion in the dye bath. The tension acts as a resist, creating sunburst-like patterns."

Textile Marbling

A surface design technique that mimics the appearance of natural marble stone. Dyes are floated on a liquid base and swirled into patterns before the fabric is laid on top to "pick up" the beautiful, feathered design.

Binding

Binding is a classic tie-dye method where sections of the fabric are tightly wrapped with thread or rubber bands. The resulting pattern creates a structured, repeating geometric effect, often referred to as a 'spider' or 'shibori' style.

Sponging Technique

This involves using a natural or synthetic sponge instead of a brush to apply paint. By dabbing lightly, you create a stippled or mottled texture, excellent for soft backgrounds, cloudy effects, or a vintage look.

Filling Technique

This technique involves applying a solid, uniform layer of color to a specific enclosed area of a design. It requires a steady hand and a well-loaded brush to ensure the paint is distributed evenly without streaks.

Grading & Stroke Technique

Grading: Blending colors to create a smooth transition, giving a 3D effect.
Stroke Technique: Focuses on brush movement and pressure to create fine tips and thicker bases in a single motion.

Outline Technique

Outlining is used to define the edges of a design and add intricate details. It provides a finished look and adds clarity and movement to a composition, usually done with a fine-liner brush.

Batik Printing

Batik is a wax-resist dyeing technique. A design is drawn with melted wax. The famous 'crackled' or veining effect happens when the wax cracks during dyeing, allowing tiny lines of color to seep through.

Direct Block Printing

Involves carving a design into a block of wood, dipping it in dye, and pressing it onto fabric. It requires great skill to align the blocks perfectly to create a continuous, balanced pattern.
